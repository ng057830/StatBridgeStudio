Ordinal Methods

How I Analyze Likert Scale Data

Likert scales are ordinal in nature and violate normality assumptions. I ensure your statistical calculations respect this constraint.

1. Coding & Reverse Scoring

I structure your dataset variables, map scale anchors (e.g., Strongly Agree to Strongly Disagree), and handle reverse-worded items to prevent bias in aggregate scores.

2. Internal Consistency (Cronbach's Alpha)

Evaluating whether your items consistently measure a latent trait is mandatory. I run reliability tests, check item-total correlation values, and identify variables that degrade reliability.

3. Descriptive Ordinal Aggregates

I calculate appropriate central tendencies. Unlike continuous data, Likert scales are summarized using medians, modes, frequencies, and interquartile ranges (IQR).

4. Non-Parametric Group Testing

When testing differences across ordinal categories, standard parametric tests (like t-tests) can bias results. I utilize Mann-Whitney U, Wilcoxon signed-rank, or Kruskal-Wallis tests.

5. Ordinal Association Checks

I compute non-parametric correlation matrices (Spearman's rho, Kendall's tau-b) to measure the direction and strength of associations between ordinal scale indicators.

6. Visualizing Likert Distributions

I create clean charts to display scale response spreads (stacked bar charts, frequency histograms) to make respondent patterns easy to read in your report.
